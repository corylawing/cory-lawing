02 / Process

How engagements
actually start.

I don't pitch retainers on first calls. Trust gets built before commitment does — and the engagement model is designed around that. Most clients come in through step one and never feel pushed into step three before they're ready.

  1. 01

    ~30 min · no commitment

    Free intro call

    A 30-minute conversation, no slides. The goal is to understand what you're actually trying to solve and figure out honestly whether I can help. If I'm not the right fit, I'll usually know someone who is.

  2. 02

    2–8 weeks · fixed fee

    Paid audit or build sprint

    If we both want to keep going, we agree on a fixed-scope, fixed-fee engagement. Two paths depending on what you actually need:

    • auditYou don't yet know what to build. I run a diagnostic deep-dive across systems, data, and workflow, and deliver a prioritised roadmap.
    • buildYou already know. I scope and ship the thing — a custom app, an integration, an n8n + AI workflow, a Salesforce build. Working software, not slides.

    We pick the right track on the intro call. Either way, low risk, real work, no hand-waving.

  3. 03

    monthly · only when earned

    Ongoing partnership

    Embedded retainer engagements happen when the first project makes the case for them — never before. Whether we started with an audit or a build, by step three we both already know how the work goes and what the value looks like. The partnership earns itself before it begins.

04 / Approach

How the work
actually runs.

Four principles that shape every engagement. Some come from architecture, some from leading commercial teams; together they're the reason the systems still work two years later.

i.

Diagnose before you design

Most replatforms fail because the diagnosis was actually a wishlist. The first weeks are about understanding the revenue motion — not the screen-deep symptoms. Real constraints, real data, real cadence.

ii.

Architecture is a business decision, not an IT one

Object models, sharing, automation strategy, integration topology — these aren't decisions you punt to engineering. They quietly govern what each function can deliver, how fast it can adapt, and what the rest of the company gets to see on Monday morning. I architect like the business depends on it. Because it does.

iii.

Bulkify by default, govern by design

Governor limits, security review, technical debt, observability — the boring half of the work is the half that decides whether what you ship survives the next quarter of growth. I write code that scales and processes that explain themselves, on Salesforce and off it.

iv.

Leave the team stronger

Embedded doesn't mean external. The point of the engagement is that your admin, your AEs, your CRO are more capable when I leave the room than when I arrived. Documentation, enablement, and a real handover plan from day one.
